# The Public Rooms – Performance

.ve-media https://edisondigital.rutgers.edu/iiif/nv10085 right width=60% seq=2

The Reception Hall and Reception Room functioned less as rooms of occasional gathering than as continuous sites of display. Layered Middle Eastern rugs, carved oak furniture, silk velvet hangings, framed paintings, ceramics, and polished metal objects created a dense visual field that appeared static and effortless. Yet this apparent stillness depended on daily intervention. Dust settled quickly on carved surfaces and gilt frames; textiles shifted with air currents and use; rugs required brushing to maintain pile and alignment. These rooms were not simply decorated—they were curated. Servants moved through them carefully each morning, straightening, brushing, adjusting, and inspecting so that the illusion of permanence could be sustained. The labor was constant but deliberately unobtrusive, producing an environment that looked untouched.
That maintenance required a specific type of work: low-intensity, repetitive vigilance rather than heavy exertion. Servants dusted without disturbing arrangements, handled objects without leaving marks, and preserved rather than transformed. Importantly, the boundaries of that labor were clearly defined. While household staff performed the daily stabilization of these luxury materials, the most invasive forms of cleaning—washing fine textiles, deep rug cleaning, major polishing of metals, or repairing decorative finishes—were withheld from them and entrusted to outside specialists. Luxury did not reduce labor; it redistributed it into attentiveness, restraint, and specialized knowledge. The more elaborate the display, the more carefully servants had to manage their own presence within it.

Consisted of the library, where guests would sign in, the reception hall, which was used by guests, and the drawing room The formal front rooms of houses like Glenmont required continual maintenance in order to project refinement, wealth, and social respectability. Spaces such as the Drawing Room and Reception Room were filled with decorative textiles, upholstered furniture, rugs, lamps, framed artwork, and ornamental objects that demanded regular care. Curtains, portières, lambrequins, and other heavy fabrics collected dust easily and required brushing, shaking outdoors, or seasonal cleaning to preserve their appearance. Oriental rugs and animal skins had to be swept, rotated, and protected from fading or moth damage, while marble mantels, polished woodwork, mirrors, and metal fixtures required constant dusting and polishing. Lighting fixtures also demanded attention, whether kerosene lamps that required wick trimming and cleaning or electric fixtures with delicate glass shades that accumulated soot and dust. By the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, middle- and upper-class homeowners increasingly relied on professional decorators, upholsterers, and furnishing firms to shape these interiors. While women like Mina Edison often directed household aesthetics and made decisions about taste and arrangement, they frequently worked alongside professional firms that supplied textiles, coordinated furnishings, and supervised redecorating projects. The upkeep of these elaborate interiors depended upon continuous domestic labor that maintained the carefully curated appearance expected of elite homes during this period.
